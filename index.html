Why StarForge?

The future of spaceflight will be built in space, not launched from Earth.

In-space manufacturing is crucial for sustainable long-term space activities: launching mass from Earth is prohibitively expensive. Orbital production platforms are essential both to gather real process data today and to produce goods in relevant quantities tomorrow. The first such facilities will be realised in Low Earth Orbit – and Starlab is a key stepping stone.

Aboard Starlab, the proposed StarForge platform will demonstrate and evaluate laser-based sintering, melting, and cutting processes. It targets metallurgy, polymer and in-space manufacturing research, while also serving as a crew support system capable of producing custom hardware on demand.

By fabricating objects directly from digital inputs, StarForge also opens up powerful opportunities for public outreach and education, providing a safe and engaging interface between citizens and space activities.

Key Capabilities

The StarForge Platform

A compact, modular in-space production system for Starlab.

MOPA Fibre Laser Core

StarForge is built around a MOPA fibre laser system (200 – 2000 W) with galvanometer scanner. This enables fast, versatile beam control with minimal system complexity to process powders for additive and subtractive manufacturing.

  • High power, tunable laser output
  • Fast beam steering via galvo scanner
  • Optimised for powder-based processes

Autonomous Intelligence

An integrated AI system supports unsupervised runs. It monitors layer quality and detects faults using machine learning techniques tailored to large datasets and pattern recognition.

  • Vision-based process monitoring
  • Automatic anomaly logging
  • Escalation to ground if needed

Post-Processing & Recycling

StarForge integrates post-processing and recycling to close the material loop:

  • Automated bandsaw detaches parts from the build plate
  • Pulveriser recycles obsolete objects into powder feedstock
  • Supports circular material use and logistical autonomy

Scientific & Technical Relevance

Connecting materials science, sustainability, ISRU and AI.

1

Materials Research & Processes

The platform enables microgravity studies on powders (steel, aluminium, polymers, and later regolith simulants), advancing the fundamental understanding of sintering, flow, and solidification processes in space environments.

Combining additive and subtractive processes unlocks powerful synergies: 3D printing provides structural flexibility, while laser cutting delivers smoother edges and tighter tolerances than 3D printing alone.

2

Sustainability

The integrated shredding and reprocessing unit closes the material loop by converting scrap into reusable powder feedstock. This supports circular use of materials – a key requirement for long-duration missions and closed habitats.

3

ISRU Pathway

By preparing the system for regolith-based feedstocks, the platform becomes a direct stepping stone toward in-situ resource utilisation (ISRU) on the Moon, Mars, and asteroids.

4

Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence enables vision-based autonomous fault detection throughout the entire production and scrapping process. This minimises the need for crew interventions and ground-based monitoring, ensuring optimal efficiency and reducing operational costs.

Challenges & Solutions

Managing powders, heat, and laser safety in a crewed orbital station.

The major technical challenge lies in the safe handling of powdered feedstocks. The powder must remain confined and must not escape into the cabin through air or finished products.

  • Controlled airflow & filtration: all airflow is filtered to ensure no particles escape. Active airflow in the locked machine removes residual surface material from finished parts.
  • Electrostatic & magnetic control: in microgravity, electrostatic and magnetic forces are used to control material deposition and positioning during printing.
  • Sealed process chambers: redundant containment layers protect crew and station systems from metallic dust and support emergency shutdown procedures.

Multiple subsystems generate heat: the laser, drives and motors, and the grinding process in the recycler.

  • Thermal simulations: identify hot spots during operation and inform design and assembly choices.
  • Integrated cooling: cooling systems ensure all components remain in their operational temperature range, leveraging active air control for dust handling.
  • Starlab interface: heat is transferred to the MDLE heat exchange interface of Starlab.

Safe handling of the high-power laser is essential for both astronaut safety and station integrity.

  • Closed compartments: no laser light can leave the machine; emergency laser shutdown is triggered if any opening is detected.
  • Self-locking doors & status LEDs: prevent accidental access during hazardous operations.
  • Redundant containment: multiple layers of protection ensure no single failure leads to leakage of laser light into the station.

State of the Art

Building on proven terrestrial technology, adapted for Starlab.

StarForge relies on mature, well-established components extensively validated under terrestrial conditions: high-power fibre lasers, beam delivery and scanning optics, powder recycling systems, AI-based defect detection, and dust containment and filtration units. These subsystems form a robust foundation for adapting the architecture to microgravity.

Additive manufacturing in space has already been demonstrated aboard the ISS, starting with polymers and advancing towards metals and regolith-based materials. Most systems use filament-based extrusion, simplifying material handling but limiting geometric precision and material flexibility.

StarForge’s Advance

StarForge pursues a powder-based laser approach that offers:

  • Higher precision and structural complexity
  • Support for metals and regolith simulants
  • Integrated additive & subtractive processing
  • In-situ recycling of produced parts
  • Autonomous operation in orbit

A fully integrated system combining manufacturing and recycling is a novel step towards industrial-grade orbital infrastructure.

Business Case

Enabling sustainable, economically viable manufacturing in orbit.

Customers & Revenue Streams

StarForge targets both upstream and downstream applications, opening multiple revenue streams while addressing critical needs for Starlab and future orbital platforms.

  • Space agencies and research institutions: material studies and process data
  • Private companies: custom material samples and structural components
  • Crew support: in-orbit production of custom hardware and spare parts

Reducing crew involvement is key to keeping operations cost-effective. AI-based fault detection and remote monitoring allow autonomous operation, with crew only intervening for sample retrieval or exceptional maintenance.

Circular & Scalable

A central differentiator is StarForge’s emphasis on circularity: internal recycling significantly reduces or eliminates the need for consumable replenishment.

  • Closed-loop business model where internal recycling makes power consumption the main cost driver.
  • For open use cases (where parts leave the system), pricing reflects feedstock resupply costs.
  • Future capability to convert packaging waste or polymers into usable feedstock.

No known competitor currently targets this segment. While many in-space manufacturing efforts focus on high-value, low-mass products (pharma, semiconductors), StarForge pioneers functional and structural manufacturing with in-situ utility and logistical sustainability – as a first mover in a critical domain.

The Photonic Fabricators

A multidisciplinary team with flight heritage and outreach experience.

The Photonic Fabricators team comprises seven members from Stuttgart, Zurich, and Geneva, combining technical, strategic, and human-centred design skills relevant to in-space manufacturing.

  • Aerospace engineering, automation, and digital innovation from the University of Stuttgart
  • Digital business development, design thinking, and human-centred design from ZHAW
  • Experience from institutions such as DLR, ESA Clean Space, and CERN

Flight Heritage & Outreach

Team members have served as Principal Investigators and engineers for:

  • Two ISS experiments within the DLR Überflieger Programme
  • Three sounding rocket missions in the REXUS/BEXUS Programme
  • Projects and internships at OHB, Airbus, ATMOS Space Cargo, HyImpulse and others

The team also has a strong track record in science communication, having successfully conducted outreach campaigns linked to past spaceflight experiments – a foundation for engaging the public with StarForge’s digital-to-orbital manufacturing capabilities.
